SUPPLEMENTARY INFORMATION
A phase diagram for jammed matter
Chaoming Song, Ping Wang, Hernán A. Makse

Here, we describe the details of the calculations leading to the phase diagram of jammed
matter. The main theoretical result is the definition of the volume function based on the
Voronoi volume of a particle explained in Section I. Section II describes the isostatic con-
dition that defines the ensemble of jammed matter through the ΘW function. Section III
explains the difference between the geometrical, z, and mechanical, Z, coordination number,
which is important to define the canonical partition function. Section IV defines the parti-
tion function for the ensemble of jammed states under the isostatic condition, Qiso. Section
V explains how to solve the partition function Qiso which leads to the equations of state (4)
and (5), and the phase diagram of Fig. 1. Finally, Section VI explains the numerical studies
to test the theoretical predictions.

I. VOLUME FUNCTION
The aim of this section is to delineate the statistical theory for calculating the free volume,
W, associated with each particle. First we find an analytical formula for the Voronoi volume
of each particle. Then a statistical theory finds that the volume function is related to the
coordination number:
W(z) = κ/z * Vg, where κ = 2√3.
This free volume function is then used in the canonical partition function to calculate the
phase diagram of jammed states.

A. Microscopic volume function
The definition of a Voronoi cell is a convex polygon whose interior consists of all points
closer to a given particle than to any other.

Formally, the volume of the Voronoi cell of particle i is:
Wvor_i = (1/d) ∫ (li(s))^d ds,
where li(s) = min_j rij / (2 s·r̂ij), and the integration is over all directions s. The formula
relates Voronoi volume to the minimal projection distance among neighbors.

This expression shows the Voronoi volume depends strongly on global positional correlations.
Thus a coarse-grained approximation is introduced: the orientational volume Ws_i:
Ws_i = Vg * ( (1/(2R)) * min_j rij / (s·r̂ij) )^d.

For isotropic packings, Ws_i can be used without orientational averaging, because the orienta-
tion is arbitrary. The reduced free volume function is:
w = ⟨ Ws_i − Vg ⟩ / Vg.

B. Mesoscopic volume function
We compute the distribution of c = r / cosθ over all neighbors j. The mesoscopic free volume
is:
w = ∫_0^1 (c^3 − 1) dP>(c),
where P>(c) = PB(c) PC(c) is the probability all neighbors satisfy r/(cosθ) > c.

Background probability:
PB(c) = exp( − V*(c) / (Vg w) ),
where V*(c) = Vg [ (c^3 − 1) − 3(1 − 1/c) ].

Contact probability:
PC(c) = exp( − sqrt(3)/2 * z * (1 − 1/c) ).

Thus:
P>(c) = exp( − [ (c^3 −1) − 3(1−1/c) ]/w  − sqrt(3)/2 * z(1 − 1/c) ).

Solving the integral leads to a closed-form self-consistent solution:
w = 2√3 / z,
or equivalently:
W = 2√3 Vg / z.

This is the mesoscopic “Hamiltonian” volume function used for statistical mechanics.

C. Tests
The exponential ansatz for PB(c) and PC(c) agrees reasonably with MD simulations at the
frictionless point. Deviations exist, but the mean ⟨Ws⟩ is accurate, giving good φ predictions.

II. ISOSTATIC CONDITION
For N particles, coordination number Z = 2M/N. Variables:
- Nn normal forces
- Nt tangential forces
- Ef force balance eqns
- Et torque balance eqns.

Isostaticity: Nn + Nt = Ef + Et.

For dimension d, friction coefficient μ, and fractions f1(μ), f2(μ):
Z(μ) = 2d ( 1 + ½(d−1) f1(μ) ) / ( 1 + (d−1) f2(μ) ).
This yields:
Z = 2d for frictionless,
Z = d+1 for infinitely rough spheres.

III. GEOMETRICAL vs MECHANICAL COORDINATION
z = geometrical coordination (contacts in the Voronoi sense)
Z = mechanical coordination (contacts carrying force)

Always: Z ≤ z ≤ 6.

Example: μ→∞ gives Z=4 mechanically, but geometry still gives z≈6.

Definitions using modified g_z(r):
gz(r) counts the number of neighbors contacting a virtual sphere of radius r.

Simulations show:
- gz(0) = mechanical Z
- gz( small r ) → constant z ≈ 6 for all RCP packings.

Coarse-grained coordination h z_i,l shows Gaussian distributions whose mean converges to z.

IV. PARTITION FUNCTION
Edwards ensemble:
Q = ∫ g(W) exp(−W/X) Θ_W dW.
Since W = κ/z, change variables:
g(W) = ∫ P(W|z) g(z) dz,
P(W|z) = δ(W − κ/z),
g(z) ∝ (h_z)^z (density of states decreases with larger z).

Thus:
Q_iso(X,Z) = ∫_{z=Z}^{6} (h_z)^z exp( − κ/(z X) ) dz,
where κ = 2√3 Vg.

V. EQUATIONS OF STATE
Average volume fraction:
φ^{-1} = 1 + W/Vg = 1 + κ/z.

Thus:
φ(X,Z) = [1/Q_iso] ∫ z/(z + κ) exp( − κ/(zX) + z ln(h_z) ) dz.

Limits:
X = 0 selects z=6 only:
φ_RCP = 6 / (6 + 2√3) ≈ 0.634.

X→∞ selects minimal z=Z:
φ_RLP(Z) ≈ Z/(Z + 2√3).

Entropy:
S(X,Z) = ⟨W⟩/X + ln Q_iso.

VI. SIMULATIONS
Molecular dynamics with Hertz–Mindlin forces:
- Fn = (2/3) kn R^{1/2} δ^{3/2}
- ΔFt = kt (R δ)^{1/2} Δs
with Coulomb Ft ≤ μ Fn.

Preparation: compress gas-like initial state to φ1, relax, check jamming. Use bisection to find
φc with tolerance 2 × 10^{-4}. Simulations confirm:
- RCP line exists for all μ
- z ≈ 6 along RCP
- mechanical Z varies with μ
- RLP minimum φ ≈ 0.539.

Packings with same φi fall on same isocompactivity curves.
