Packing of Compressible Granular Materials
Hernán A. Makse, David L. Johnson, and Lawrence M. Schwartz

3D computer simulations and experiments are employed to study random packings of compressible spherical grains under external confining stress. In the rigid ball limit, we find a continuous transition in which the stress vanishes as σ ∼ (φ − φ_c)^β, where φ is the solid-phase volume density. The value of φ_c depends on whether the grains interact via only normal forces (giving rise to random close packings) or by a combination of normal and friction-generated transverse forces (producing random loose packings). In both cases, near the transition, the system’s response is controlled by localized force chains.

Dense packings of spherical particles are an important starting point for the study of simple liquids, metallic glasses, colloids, biological systems, and granular matter. In liquids and glasses, finite-temperature molecular dynamics studies of hard-sphere models show a first-order liquid–solid transition as φ increases. Above freezing, a metastable disordered state persists until φ ≈ φ_RCP, the random close packing density.

Granular packings at zero temperature behave differently: interparticle forces are nonlinear, repulsive, and history-dependent. Conventional continuum elasto-plastic approaches have been challenged; alternative approaches argue granular packings form “fragile matter” requiring exotic closure relations. Microscopic studies (contact dynamics, q-models) often neglect deformability.

Here, deformable grains are essential. We study deformable spheres with Hertz–Mindlin contacts. Deformability allows φ > φ_RCP (hard sphere limit) and enables studying the approach to RCP and random loose packings (RLP). The rigid-grain limit is a continuous phase transition where stress vanishes continuously as (φ − φ_c)^β.

Crucially, φ_c depends on force laws:
– Normal forces only → φ_c ≈ 0.634 (RCP)
– Normal + tangential friction forces → φ_c ≈ 0.6284 (RLP)

Simulations show transitions near both RCP and RLP are driven by localized force chains.

Numerical Simulations:
We perform 3D discrete element simulations (DEM) with Hertz–Mindlin normal and tangential contact forces plus viscous dissipation. Grain material: shear modulus 29 GPa, ν = 0.2, radius 0.1 ± 0.005 mm.

Initial state: gas of 10,000 nonoverlapping spheres in a cube (4 mm side), periodic boundaries. Apply isotropic compression/expansion until reaching φ slightly below φ_c → zero pressure and zero coordination. Then compress uniaxially until reaching target vertical stress σ and coordination Z.

Results:
Stress vs φ:
σ → 0 at φ_c ≈ 0.6284 for frictional grains. Stress fits power law:
σ ∼ (φ − φ_c)^β, with β ≈ 1.6 ± 0.2.

For frictionless grains:
φ_c ≈ 0.634 ± 0.004 (RCP), β ≈ 2.0 ± 0.2.

Coordination Z vs φ:
Z − Z_c ∼ (φ − φ_c)^u.
At frictional critical state: Z_c ≈ 4, u ≈ 0.29.
At frictionless critical state: Z_c ≈ 6, u ≈ 0.94.

These Z_c values match constraint-counting/isostaticity arguments:
– Frictional spheres: Z_c = D + 1 = 4
– Frictionless spheres: Z_c = 2D = 6

The φ_c ≈ 0.6284 frictional result matches random loose packing (RLP). The frictionless result matches RCP, consistent with experiments.

Force Distributions:
At low stress, force distributions are exponential:
P(f) ∝ exp(−f / ⟨f⟩),
consistent with “force-chain” dominated states.

At higher compression, distributions become Gaussian:
P(f) ∼ exp(−(f − f_0)^2 / k^2),
indicating homogenized force networks.

This exponential → Gaussian transition is observed for both frictional and frictionless systems and is robust to preparation.

Localization:
Force chains dominate near φ_c. To quantify localization, define participation number:

P = (Σ_i q_i^2)^{-1},
where q_i = f_i / Σ_j f_j.

Interpretation:
– P = 1 → uniform force distribution.
– P → 0 → extreme localization (few chains carry all load).

Results:
P increases with σ:
For σ < 2.1 MPa: P ∼ log σ.
For σ > 2.1 MPa: P saturates at P ≈ 0.62.

This suggests a delocalization transition as compression increases. At high σ, nearly all grains carry comparable loads.

Idealized force-chain model:
Assume N_FC chains, each with N_z spheres. Each sphere has:
– 2 strong load-bearing vertical contacts
– ≈4 weak lateral contacts carrying fraction a < 1 of load

Leads to approximate formula:
P ≳ 2⟨Z⟩ (1 + 2a^2)^2 / [ (1 + 2a^2)^2 N_FC N_z ].

For ⟨Z⟩ ≈ 8 and a ≈ 2/5, P ≈ 0.62, matching saturation value.

Visualization:
Force-chain extraction by tracing strongest contact per grain shows:
– Near φ_c: sparse, localized chains percolate system.
– At higher stress: dense interconnected network forming an elastic-like homogeneous medium.

Experiments:
Carbon-paper experiments with glass beads (diameter 0.8 ± 0.05 mm) reproduce simulation conclusions:
– For σ < 750 kPa: force distribution exponential.
– For σ > 750 kPa: distribution becomes Gaussian.

Conclusion:
Using simulations and experiments, we observe a continuous phase transition as φ → φ_c (RLP or RCP). Near φ_c:
– Stress vanishes as (φ − φ_c)^β
– Coordination approaches minimal Z_c (4 frictional, 6 frictionless)
– Force networks are fragile, dominated by sparse force chains
– Force distributions are exponential

At larger compression:
– New contacts form
– Localization disappears
– Force distributions become Gaussian
– Participation saturates, indicating homogeneous stress transmission.

This crossover signifies a transition from a fragile, chain-supported structure to an elastic, homogenized granular solid.
